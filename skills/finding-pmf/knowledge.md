# Finding Product-Market Fit - Knowledge Base

> Curated from 15+ episodes of Lenny's Podcast

## Frameworks

### 1. The Sean Ellis Test - Sean Ellis

Ask users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" If 40%+ say "very disappointed," you have PMF. Critical insight: ignore the "somewhat disappointed" users -- they're telling you it's a nice-to-have. Building for them dilutes your core value.

*Source: episodes/sean-ellis*

### 2. Pattern Breakers Framework - Mike Maples Jr

Breakthrough startups have three elements:
- **Inflections** -- market shifts enabling asymmetric competition
- **Insights** -- secrets about what's missing
- **Founder-future fit** -- why this founder for this future

80% of Maples' best returns came from pivots, not original plans.

*Source: episodes/mike-maples-jr*

### 3. Viral Insights Framework - Rahul Vohra (via LinkedIn's Elliot Shmukler)

True virality isn't built through viral mechanics (those max out at 0.7-0.9 viral coefficient). Real growth comes from word-of-mouth -- people spontaneously telling others. Focus on building something remarkable.

*Source: episodes/rahul-vohra*

### 4. The Activation-First Approach - Sean Ellis

PMF is meaningless if users never experience your core value. At LogMeIn, 95% of signups never used the product. Freezing all feature work and fixing activation (5% to 50%) unlocked everything: same channels went from $10K/month to $1M/month.

*Source: episodes/sean-ellis*

## Common Mistakes

1. **Optimizing for "somewhat disappointed" users early on.** These users represent nice-to-haves. Tweaking product based on their feedback dilutes the core value for must-have users. (Sean Ellis)

2. **Launching with inadequate activation.** LogMeIn had 95% drop-off before users ever experienced the product. Customer acquisition is meaningless if people don't activate. (Sean Ellis)

3. **Pursuing "tar pit" ideas.** Seductive startup ideas that seem unsolved but have been attempted repeatedly since the 1990s. They draw you in and are nearly impossible to pivot out of. (Dalton Caldwell)

4. **Spending too much time on craft in speed-dependent markets.** For network-effect marketplaces, speed is critical. For mission-critical products like email, quality is non-negotiable. Know your market. (Rahul Vohra)

## Company Examples

**Superhuman** (Rahul Vohra): Deliberately ignored most early user feedback. Manually onboarded every user for years (20 people doing manual onboarding at peak). Spent enormous time on details before launch because email is mission-critical.

**LogMeIn** (Sean Ellis): 5% activation rate. CEO froze entire roadmap for 3 months. Result: 50% activation. Same acquisition channels went from $10K/month to $1M/month. Word-of-mouth jumped to 80% of new users.

**Mailbox/Dropbox** (Rahul Vohra): Acquired for ~$100M, popularized "swipe to archive." But launched with bugs that lost emails. Cautionary tale: good ideas fail if they don't work reliably in mission-critical contexts.
