# Growth Frameworks - Knowledge Base

> Curated from 38+ episodes of Lenny's Podcast

## Frameworks

### 1. Hierarchy of Engagement - Sarah Tavel

Three-level framework for consumer products:
- **Level 1:** Identify the core action (the single most important user behavior)
- **Level 2:** Retention -- make the product get better the more you use it (accruing benefits)
- **Level 3:** Self-perpetuation through network effects, viral loops, and re-engagement

*Source: episodes/sarah-tavel*

### 2. Kindle vs. Fire Strategies - Casey Winters

Early growth uses non-scalable "kindle" hacks to unlock "fire" strategies -- the scalable loops (content, sales, viral, paid) that drive millions of users. Only hire dedicated growth roles once you've proven which fire strategy works.

*Source: episodes/casey-winters*

### 3. Black Loops and Blue Loops - Shishir Mehrotra (Coda)

Two distinct growth mechanisms:
- **Black Loop:** Users share with collaborators who create their own (sharing -> creating -> sharing). How Microsoft Office spreads.
- **Blue Loop:** Publishing to the world. Users create content publicly and people discover the product through great ideas.

*Source: episodes/shishir-mehrotra*

### 4. Distribution Platform Cycle - Brian Balfour

How new platforms emerge and evolve:
- **Step 0:** Consensus on category, multiple competitors
- **Step 1:** Identify moat, build third-party platform
- **Step 2:** Open platform gives away distribution
- **Step 3:** Closing down, monetization through ads

Companies that recognize early steps in new platforms have massive opportunities.

*Source: episodes/brian-balfour*

### 5. Golden Rituals - Shishir Mehrotra (citing Bing Gordon)

Great companies have a small list of golden rituals with three rules: (1) they're named, (2) every employee knows them by their first Friday, (3) they're templated. Examples: Amazon's 6-pagers, Google's OKRs.

*Source: episodes/shishir-mehrotra*

## Common Mistakes

1. **Premature scaling before PMF.** Founders focus on acquisition before fixing onboarding and activation. "Customer acquisition is so hard that if you're not efficient at converting and retaining, you're going to struggle." (Sean Ellis, Casey Winters)

2. **Building too many features at once.** Founders compare themselves to mature products and try to build everything, creating cognitive overload. Under-invest in Level 1 (the core action). (Sarah Tavel)

3. **Ignoring the platform closing phase.** Companies build entirely on new platforms without understanding they'll eventually require paid distribution. (Brian Balfour)

4. **Operations as a band-aid.** "Having marketing ops means you suck at marketing. Ops is a sign of inefficiency." Use automation to eliminate the need for ops. (Casey Winters)

## Company Examples

**Pinterest** (Sarah Tavel): Built "Picked for You" algorithmic feed where every pin saved personalized the experience. Created a network effect: more pins = better recommendations = more to lose by leaving.

**Dropbox** (Sean Ellis): Improved activation from 5% to 50%, then same acquisition channels went from $10K/month to $1M/month. Referral program accelerated already-existing word-of-mouth.

**TikTok** (Sarah Tavel): Spent $1B+ on paid acquisition -- but only AFTER nailing retention. They didn't spend on ads until the product was retentive.

**Facebook Platform** (Brian Balfour): Launched third-party platform, attracted massive developer ecosystems (Zynga), gave distribution, then gradually closed it down. Textbook four-step cycle.
