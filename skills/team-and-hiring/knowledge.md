# Team Building & Hiring - Knowledge Base

> Curated from 49+ episodes of Lenny's Podcast

## Frameworks

### 1. PSHE Framework (Problem, Solution, How, Execution) - Shishir Mehrotra

Evaluate talent across all levels:
- **Junior:** Handed the problem, solution, and how -- they execute
- **Mid:** Given problem and solution -- they figure out how
- **Senior:** Given the problem -- they propose solutions
- **Most senior:** They define the problems

Works beyond product: applies to engineers, salespeople, all roles.

*Source: episodes/shishir-mehrotra*

### 2. "Best-in-Class Company" Hiring Playbook - Gokul Rajaram

When hiring leaders: identify the top 3-4 companies that excel at the function you need (in a similar customer segment), then hire their "lieutenants" -- not the top leader. Avoids poaching visible leaders and brings in proven talent from winning cultures.

*Source: episodes/gokul-rajaram*

### 3. Levels and Ladders Framework - Claire Hughes Johnson (ex-COO Stripe)

Build explicit salary bands and levels early (around 160 people). Do it earlier than you think -- waiting until 800 people makes it a "blood bath." Minimize early titles (avoid director/VP at 25-person companies) to focus on scope and impact.

*Source: episodes/claire-hughes-johnson*

### 4. DRI + AORs (Directly Responsible Individual + Areas of Responsibility) - Emily Kramer

Create a list mapping who owns what -- not job titles, but specific responsibilities. This clarity eliminates confusion, prevents silos, and scales as you break down responsibilities over time.

*Source: episodes/emily-kramer*

### 5. Reference Checks Over Interviews - Shishir Mehrotra

"I generally value the reference check over interview signals. If I had to stack rank, the reference check is top of the list. Those people worked with this person for years. What you get out of 30 minutes of artificial scenarios is never going to compare."

*Source: episodes/shishir-mehrotra*

## Common Mistakes

1. **Promoting based on scope alone.** Only rewarding scope incentivizes people away from risky, creative projects. Evaluate the "how" as much as scale. (Shishir Mehrotra)

2. **Hiring based on title/experience from competitors.** Many founders hire the obvious person from a competitor, missing better talent in adjacent industries. Think best-in-class companies first. (Gokul Rajaram)

3. **Giving director/VP titles too early.** These titles create the most contention and are hard to reverse. A 25-person company shouldn't have a VP of Engineering -- when you layer, you've already promised something you can't deliver. (Gokul Rajaram)

4. **Relying on process over people.** Teams do "splatergy" (busy work) not strategy. Also: implementing cascading OKRs too early slows everything. Balance is "go slow upfront to go fast later." (Emily Kramer)

## Company Examples

**Square** (Gokul Rajaram): Eliminated title-based contention entirely. Jack Dorsey saw Twitter titles being weaponized and started a scope-focused culture at Square. "Never had a single discussion with my team about what the title should be."

**Stripe** (Claire Hughes Johnson): Built hiring processes, ladders, and operations from 160 to 7,000+ people. Talked to Square and Airbnb first -- one said implementing levels was a "blood bath," another said waiting too long is worse.

**Slack** (Merci Grace): Stewart Butterfield's early decision "This is a tool for work" made "thousands of small decisions instant and obvious." This clarity cascaded through hiring and product.

**Dropbox** (Melissa Tan): Extremely selective about hiring first-principles thinkers and collaborative people, which drove innovation in product-led growth and sales motion.
