# Storytelling & Influence - Knowledge Base

> Curated from 17+ episodes of Lenny's Podcast

## Frameworks

### 1. "What Is, What Could Be, New Bliss" - Nancy Duarte

Three-part presentation structure: establish the current state, contrast with an imagined future, conclude with the desired outcome. Creates longing through contrast. Works from casual conversations to keynote presentations.

*Source: episodes/nancy-duarte*

### 2. Strategic Narrative Framework - Andy Raskin

Five steps:
1. Name the shift from old game to new game
2. Name the stakes -- show winners and losers
3. Name the object of the new game (e.g., "turn customers into subscribers")
4. Show the obstacles preventing success
5. Explain how to overcome those obstacles

Replaces the "arrogant doctor" pitch (problem -> solution -> why we're better).

*Source: episodes/andy-raskin*

### 3. Radical Candor Framework - Kim Scott

A 2x2 matrix: "care personally" (vertical) x "challenge directly" (horizontal).
- **Radical Candor:** Both dimensions
- **Obnoxious Aggression:** Challenge without care
- **Ruinous Empathy:** Care without challenge (90% of mistakes live here)
- **Manipulative Insincerity:** Neither

*Source: episodes/kim-scott*

### 4. Five-Second Moment of Transformation - Matthew Dicks

Every compelling story centers on a singular moment where someone's perspective shifts. All context (98% of the narrative) serves to build clarity around this one moment. Applies to movies, pitches, and anecdotes equally.

*Source: episodes/matthew-dicks*

### 5. Most Obvious Objection (MOO) Framework - Wes Kao

Before pitching, anticipate the 2-3 most obvious objections and address them proactively. Prevents meetings from feeling defensive and signals thorough thinking.

*Source: episodes/wes-kao*

## Best Quotes

> "I can get my husband to do chores for me on the weekends with a real quick 'what is, what could be, new bliss.' The ability to just have that contrast as a framework in your brain during a meeting, on a phone call, any moment of influence -- literally it works."
> -- Nancy Duarte

> "The way I learned how to pitch in business school is what I call the arrogant doctor. You have a problem, I have a solution. Every movie starts with some kind of shift in the world, and I call this the shift from the old game to a new game."
> -- Andy Raskin

> "Our minds are not designed to remember a pie chart or facts or statistics or platitudes. The risk you take if you're not telling stories is that you will be forgotten. 100%. You will be forgotten."
> -- Matthew Dicks

> "Everyone loves the word storytelling in business. But when they come to me, they don't really want to be storytellers. Because to be a storyteller means you have to separate yourself from the herd."
> -- Matthew Dicks

> "Radical Candor is just what happens when you care personally and challenge directly at the same time. By far and away the most common problem is ruinous empathy -- we're so worried about not hurting someone's feelings that we fail to tell them something they'd be better off knowing."
> -- Kim Scott

> "The blast radius of a poorly written memo is way bigger than most people think. If you shoot off a message in a Slack channel with 15 people and it's confusing, there's going to be a bunch of back and forth. Whereas if you had just taken another look, those 15 people would be off to the races."
> -- Wes Kao

## Common Mistakes

1. **Leading with logistics instead of why.** Diving into process, deadlines, and how-to without first getting buy-in on why it matters. (Wes Kao)

2. **Assuming the audience already agrees.** Even if someone agreed two weeks ago, they've thought about a billion other things. You still need brief selling context before the ask. (Wes Kao)

3. **Front-loading all stakes.** Storytellers throw everything dramatic at the beginning out of fear. Stakes should build throughout with new tensions introduced periodically. (Matthew Dicks)

4. **Forgetting the audience is the hero.** Presenters position themselves as the hero rather than the audience. The presenter should be the mentor (like Obi-Wan), coming alongside the audience. (Nancy Duarte)

## Company Examples

**Salesforce** (Andy Raskin): Marc Benioff led with "Software is over... there's this new world called the cloud." Rather than comparing feature-by-feature to Siebel, Salesforce framed the entire category shift.

**Zuora** (Andy Raskin): "In the old world, businesses operated on transactions. In this new world, the subscription economy." Showed winners already winning with subscriptions, then framed the obstacle.

**Gong** (Andy Raskin): Shifted from "sales ops tool" to "Goodbye opinions, hello reality. Winners operate with data, not hunches." Exponentially increased TAM by reframing from tool to strategic necessity.

**Slack** (Matthew Dicks): Director of communications crafted a narrative rooted in a personal pandemic moment. Adding a 30-second personal anecdote transformed a corporate pitch into an emotional connection.
