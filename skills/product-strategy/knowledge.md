# Product Strategy - Knowledge Base

> Curated from 56+ episodes of Lenny's Podcast

## Frameworks

### 1. DHM Model (Delight, Hard-to-Copy, Margin Enhancement) - Gibson Biddle

A product strategy must do three things simultaneously: delight customers, be hard for competitors to copy, and enhance business margins. Reed Hastings asked Gibson at Netflix: "Can Gib delight customers?" -- leading to this three-part test for every strategic bet.

**How to apply:** For each product initiative, score it on all three dimensions. If it only delights but is easy to copy and doesn't improve margins, it's a feature, not a strategy.

*Source: episodes/gibson-biddle*

### 2. Pre-Mortem Framework - Shreyas Doshi

Imagine your project has failed six months from now. Work backward to identify what went wrong using three categories:
- **Tigers** -- actual critical threats
- **Paper Tigers** -- perceived threats that aren't real
- **Elephants** -- the big things nobody is talking about

The genius is psychological safety: teams can voice concerns without seeming negative.

*Source: episodes/shreyas-doshi*

### 3. Opportunity Solution Tree - Teresa Torres

A visual decision tree: start with an outcome at the root, branch into the opportunity space (unmet needs), then into solutions and assumption tests. The key is spending time in the problem space before jumping to solutions.

*Source: episodes/teresa-torres*

### 4. Evidence-Guided Development - Itamar Gilad

Contrasts Google+'s failed opinion-based approach (1,000-person team, pure conviction) with Gmail's tabbed inbox success (rigorous testing). The framework: balance human judgment with evidence. Neither eliminate opinion nor blindly follow data.

*Source: episodes/itamar-gilad*

### 5. Three Levels of Product Work - Shreyas Doshi

Product decisions operate at three distinct levels:
- **Impact** -- strategic value to customers and brand
- **Execution** -- how to build and ship it
- **Optics** -- visibility and awareness of the work

Most conflicts stem from team members defaulting to different levels without recognizing the mismatch.

*Source: episodes/shreyas-doshi*

## Common Mistakes

1. **Treating all tasks equally.** Ambitious PMs waste time perfecting overhead tasks when they should focus on leverage tasks. (Shreyas Doshi)

2. **Focusing on execution without asking "why."** Teams get caught in metrics discussions without asking the fundamental question: does this actually help our users? (Shreyas Doshi)

3. **Opinion-based development without evidence.** Google+ had 1,000 people and pure conviction. Gmail's tabbed inbox had a small team and rigorous testing. Guess which one worked. (Itamar Gilad)

4. **Communicating at the wrong level.** PMs default to execution level while executives think at impact level. Without recognizing this mismatch, teams "litigate the minutiae." (Shreyas Doshi)

## Company Examples

**Netflix** (Gibson Biddle): The "Perfect New Release" test promised next-day DVD delivery vs. two-week wait. Retention barely improved (4.5% to 4.45%), revealing the real value prop wasn't speed but variety. Also: auto-canceling inactive members cost $100M but built massive brand trust.

**Gmail Tabbed Inbox** (Itamar Gilad): Started with a skeptical team. Used Wizard of Oz tests, dog-fooding, and usability studies. Result: 85-88% of 1.8 billion Gmail users love it. Success came from testing rather than assuming.

**Google+** (Itamar Gilad): Massive team (1,000 people), top-down strategy, forced integration. Shut down in 2019 because people didn't actually need another social network.

**Stripe** (Shreyas Doshi): Culture of high energy, sound judgment, low ego, and small teams. Thinking bigger with Google's "$100M minimum" filter led to different prioritization at Stripe Connect.
